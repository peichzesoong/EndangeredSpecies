Bornean Orangutan

150,000

Bornean Orangutans are lost in 16 years.

Conservation Status

Critically Endangered

In the Malay language, orangutan means "man of the forest."

Orangutans are the largest arboreal mammal, spending most of their time in trees. They can pass through the branches thanks to their long, powerful arms and grasping hands and feet. These great apes are highly intelligent animals, sharing 96.4 % of our genes.

The Bornean Orangutan differs in appearance from the Sumatran orangutan, with a broader face and shorter beard and also slightly darker in color. They are found in lowland rainforests and tropical, swamp and mountain forests in Borneo and Kalimantan.


Threats

Habitat Loss

Huge tracts of forest have been cleared throughout their range and the land used for agriculture, road development, illegal timber harvesting and unsustainable logging, mining and human encroachment.

Hunting

Since orangutans are huge and slow, they are easy target for hunters. When they move into agricultural areas and destroy crops to search for food, they are killed by humans for food or in revenge.

Illegal Trade

Young orangutans are in demand for pet trades, with each animal fetching several hundred dollars in city markets on nearby islands. Studies shown that 200-500 orangutans from Indonesian Borneo enter the pet trade each year.

Forest Fire

In 1997-1998, the drainage of peat-swamp forest contributed to uncontrollable fires in Kalimantan, which lasted for 6 months. This forest fire killed up to 8,000 orang-utans.
